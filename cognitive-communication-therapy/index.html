Cognitive Communication Therapy in Kent

Specialist Speech and Language Therapy for Cognitive Communication Difficulties

Cognitive communication difficulties can affect a person's ability to communicate effectively in everyday situations. These difficulties often arise following a stroke, traumatic brain injury, brain tumour, or other neurological condition, and may impact attention, memory, organisation, problem-solving, and social communication.

At Kent Neuro Therapy, I provide specialist assessment and therapy for adults experiencing cognitive communication difficulties across Kent. Therapy is personalised to help individuals improve their communication, regain independence, and participate more confidently in work, family life, and social activities.

What Are Cognitive Communication Difficulties?

Communication relies on more than just speech and language. It also depends on cognitive skills such as attention, memory, planning, reasoning, and self-monitoring. This includes social communication skills such as eye contact, face expressions, turn-taking, and providing the right amount of information in the right context. We need all these to plan what we are going to say, and to follow and join in conversation.

When these cognitive skills are affected, communication may become more challenging, even if speech itself is clear.

Common difficulties include:

  • Losing track of conversations and topics
  • Difficulty concentrating during discussions
  • Problems remembering information or instructions
  • Difficulty organising thoughts before speaking
  • Finding it hard to stay on topic
  • Reduced awareness of communication difficulties
  • Challenges understanding humour, sarcasm, or indirect language
  • Difficulty participating in social situations
  • Problems communicating effectively at work or in everyday activities

These challenges can affect confidence, relationships, employment, and independence.

How Speech and Language Therapy Can Help

Therapy focuses on understanding how cognitive changes are affecting everyday communication and developing practical strategies to improve participation.

Treatment may include:

  • Supporting attention and concentration during conversations
  • Developing memory strategies for everyday communication
  • Supporting planning and organisation of spoken and written language
  • Improving problem-solving and reasoning skills
  • Building confidence in social communication
  • Developing personalised compensatory strategies
  • Supporting return to work where appropriate
  • Training family members and carers to support communication effectively

Therapy is evidence-based, personalised, and tailored to each individual's goals and daily life.

Who Can Benefit?

Cognitive communication therapy may benefit adults following:

  • Stroke
  • Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)
  • Brain tumour
  • Encephalitis
  • Other neurological conditions affecting cognition and communication including dementia, Multiple Sclerosis (MS), and Parkinson's

Family and Carer Support

Cognitive communication difficulties can affect the whole family. Partners and relatives may notice changes in conversation, memory, or problem-solving that can be difficult for everyone involved.

I work closely with families to develop practical communication strategies that improve understanding, reduce misunderstandings, and support meaningful participation in everyday life.

Assessment and Therapy

Assessment explores both communication skills and the cognitive abilities that support effective communication. This helps identify individual strengths, areas of difficulty, and realistic therapy goals.

Following assessment, a personalised therapy programme is developed to address the communication challenges that matter most to the individual.

Therapy can be provided in the client's home, online, or in other appropriate settings depending on circumstances and preferences.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this the same as memory therapy?

Not exactly. While memory may be one aspect of treatment, cognitive communication therapy focuses on how thinking skills affect communication in real-life situations, such as conversations, work, social interactions, and everyday activities.

Can therapy help years after a brain injury or stroke?

Yes. Many people benefit from specialist assessment and therapy months or even years after their injury. Therapy can help develop new strategies, improve confidence, and support participation in everyday life.
